Wine Networks
Digging into how wines are connected based on similarities

Have you ever tried a wine you really loved and then become hooked on this exact wine, not willing to try something new? Are your dinner friends no longer impressed? Look no further! On this website, extensive analyis has been made in order to provide you with deeper knowledge about wines, their flavours, grapes and similarities â€“ so you can find a wine just as good as your go-to wine.

All analysis is based on data (find it at the bottom of this page) scraped from Vivino.com, the world's largest online wine marketplace, and contains everything from the wine producer and region, to the most used grapes in the wine and good food pairings, to user's ratings and average prices. To keep it semi simple and stick to the most importatnt wines, this website focuses on Italian and French wines.

Back to basics

Starting off, let's have a look at what data we are dealing with. Below, you can see which wineries most of the wines in are data are from. We have kept it simple and only display the top 5 (besed on wine count) wineries in Italy and France respecitvely. Hover over the selected areas to view more details!

Now that we know where a lot of the wines are produced, let's look into the which grapes the wines are made of.

Interesting! We see that most wines from Italy are made of Primitivo while an overwhelming amount of French wines are Syrah. Both countries produce Cabernet Sauvignon, however it is more prominent in France.

How about the wine flavours?

We see that Italian wines are governed by oaky notes and French wines are more mixed between the flavours black fruit, citrus fruit, earth and microbio.

Networks - how are the wines connected?

Moving on to what we all came for, how the wines are connected based on similarities. Below we have examined how wines are connected based on their flavours. If a wine has the same flavor profile as another wine (e.g. both have main flavour oak), an edge has been added between the nodes (each node being a wine). In order to futher destinguish and categorize the wines, the nodes have been colored based on the main grape used to produce the wine. An edge is given the color of its' nodes' grape if the two nodes (wines) the edge connects, are produced from the same grape.

Graph of Italian wines

It seems the graph is split into two main groups, one mainly consisting of wines produced from Malvasia, Arneis and Trebbiano, and the other mainly consisting of all other wines. Therefore, it makes sense that the links between the two groups are not any connecting the same grapes.

Graph of French wines

The graph of French wines cannot be spit as easily, however a clear group consisting mainly of Syrah and Cabernet Sauvignon is seen in the upper left corner. More on groupings later!

In the table below, we see the sizes of above networks. Both of them contain more than 20 000 edges (connections between the wines) though only containing around 300 nodes (wines)!
